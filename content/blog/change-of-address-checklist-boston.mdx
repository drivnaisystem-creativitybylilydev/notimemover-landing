Short version: file USPS mail forwarding first, update the RMV within 30 days, then work through banks, work, and utilities. Here's the full change of address checklist, in the order that actually matters.

## USPS Mail Forwarding Comes First

Start here, before anything else on this list. Go to [usps.com/move](https://www.usps.com/manage/forward.htm) and file a change of address, either online for a small identity-verification fee or free in person at any post office. Do it 7-10 business days before you move, since forwarding doesn't kick in instantly.

Think of forwarding as a backstop. It catches whatever slips through the cracks — a magazine, a random bill you forgot about — while you work through the rest of this list. Treating it as a replacement for updating each account directly is the single most common mistake people make.

Subletting for a few months before a permanent move, or still waiting on a lease to firm up? USPS also offers temporary forwarding for as little as 15 days. Use that instead of skipping the form entirely just because "permanent" feels premature.

## How Long Does Forwarded Mail Actually Last?

Standard First Class Mail forwarding runs up to 12 months once you file a permanent change of address, with periodicals typically forwarded for a shorter 60-day window. After that, mail sent to your old address just stops arriving anywhere, so the 12-month clock isn't as generous as it sounds once you account for annual renewals, once-a-year statements, or anything that only shows up around tax season.

Piece-by-piece forwarding also takes a beat. New mail typically starts forwarding within 3-12 business days of your request being processed, a lag that catches people off guard. Expecting something time-sensitive right after your move? Confirm with the sender directly instead of trusting the forward alone.

## Do You Need to Update Your Address With the RMV?

Yes, and there's an actual deadline attached to this one. [Massachusetts law requires you to notify the RMV within 30 days of moving](https://www.mass.gov/how-to/change-your-address-with-the-rmv), and it covers your driver's license and vehicle registration as two separate records. Updating one doesn't automatically update the other, which trips people up constantly.

Do it online through your MyMassGov account rather than waiting for an in-person visit; it takes a few minutes. Miss the 30-day window and you're looking at a $35 civil citation if it ever comes up, plus the annoyance of registration or renewal notices going to an address you don't live at anymore.

Coming to Massachusetts from another state adds a second step. The RMV address update is separate from [transferring your registration and title](https://www.mass.gov/how-to/transfer-your-registration-and-title-from-out-of-state), which new residents are expected to handle fairly quickly after establishing residency here, well before those old plates would naturally expire.

## The Rest of the Change of Address Checklist: Money, Work, and Government

Once mail forwarding and the RMV are handled, work through these, roughly in order of how much it costs you if you skip them:

- Employer and HR, so your final pay stub, W-2, and any benefits paperwork land in the right place
- Banks and credit card companies, especially before your next statement cycle closes
- The IRS, using [Form 8822](https://www.irs.gov/forms-pubs/about-form-8822) if you want your federal tax records updated directly rather than waiting for your next return
- Massachusetts voter registration, which runs through the Secretary of the Commonwealth's office and has to be updated on its own; USPS and RMV changes don't carry over to it
- Auto and health insurance providers, since your rates and coverage area can both shift with your ZIP code

Each one is a five-minute task on its own. The failure mode is always the same: people knock out the big three (RMV, bank, employer) and then let the smaller stuff quietly slide for months.

## Updating Utilities and Trash Pickup in Boston

Set a disconnect date at your old place and a start date at your new one for electric, gas, and internet, ideally a few days apart so you're not without power on move-in night. In most of the city that means [Eversource](https://www.eversource.com) for electric and National Grid for gas, though the exact provider depends on your specific address.

Trash and recycling day resets when you change neighborhoods too. Boston assigns collection by zone rather than one citywide schedule, so check your new pickup day on the city's website during your first week. Guess wrong and you'll find out the hard way, with a bag sitting on the curb for six extra days.

## What About Insurance, Healthcare, and Subscriptions?

Get your renters or homeowners policy updated to the new address before move-in day, since coverage attaches to the specific property as much as it does to you. Auto insurance works the same way — your rate is calculated partly off wherever your car is garaged.

Doctors, dentists, and any specialists you see should get the update too, mostly so referrals, bills, and appointment reminders don't end up stuck in limbo. Regular prescriptions are worth handling early: transfer your pharmacy to a location near your new place during move week, while you've still got refills left, rather than scrambling once you run out.

Subscriptions, meal kits, and anything that physically ships to you are lower stakes, but worth a five-minute pass through your email inbox for "shipping address" once you're settled.

## Moving Within Boston? Update Your Resident Parking Permit Too

This one catches people who assume a Boston-to-Boston move doesn't require paperwork. It does. [Resident parking permits are issued by zone](https://www.boston.gov/departments/parking-clerk/resident-parking-permits) rather than citywide, so a move from Allston to Southie or from JP to the North End means applying fresh for a permit in your new zone. Updating the address on your existing permit isn't an option.

The city wants your RMV registration to already show the new address before it'll approve the new permit, so this genuinely goes faster if you've knocked out the RMV step first. Do the two out of order and you'll likely get bounced back to fix the registration before the permit application goes through.

## When Should You Actually Do Each Step?

Not everything needs to happen the same week. Here's a rough timeline that avoids both the last-minute scramble and the months-long slide.

| Timing | What to Handle |
|---|---|
| 1-2 weeks before move | USPS mail forwarding, notify employer/HR, update insurance policies |
| Move week | Utility disconnect/connect dates, bank and credit card address updates |
| Within 30 days after | RMV license and registration, voter registration |
| Within 30 days after (if staying in Boston) | New resident parking permit for your zone |
| First month, ongoing | Subscriptions, healthcare providers, anything lower-priority |

Crews notice a pattern on move day. Customers who already know their exact final number, because it was [locked in before the truck arrived](/blog/hidden-moving-fees-boston), tend to have this kind of list printed out or pulled up on their phone by the time the last box is off the truck. Customers still doing mental math over a surprise bill almost never do. The paperwork isn't harder for one group than the other — it's just competing with less stress.

## What Happens If You Just... Don't?

Nothing catastrophic happens in week one. That's exactly the problem — the consequences show up months later, disconnected from the move itself, so people don't connect the dots. A registration renewal notice bounces back undeliverable. A tax document goes to a stranger's mailbox. An insurance policy lapses because a renewal never reached you.

Each item is a minor crisis on its own, but they compound. Building this list into the same week as your [moving day timeline](/blog/boston-moving-day-timeline) is the difference between a clean transition and six months of small, annoying fires.
