Short answer: check for a US DOT or DPU number, insist on an in-home or video estimate, and walk away from anyone asking for a big deposit upfront. Here's what actually separates a real Boston mover from one that'll cost you more than the quote suggested.

## Start With the Number, Not the Price

Before anything else, ask what license or registration number the company operates under. Interstate movers are required to register with the [Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration and display a US DOT number](https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/protect-your-move/select-mover), and purely local Massachusetts movers fall under the state DPU instead. A company that hesitates, deflects, or can't produce this number immediately isn't worth a second conversation.

This matters more in a market like Boston than in most cities. Between the University of Massachusetts system, BU, Northeastern, and every other school driving September turnover, there's no shortage of fly-by-night operators showing up every summer to catch the seasonal rush and disappear by fall. A real registration number is the fastest way to filter them out before you've wasted an hour on a phone call.

## Never Book Off a Phone Quote Alone

A legitimate estimate requires someone to actually see what you're moving, either in person or over video. Movers who quote a flat number sight-unseen, based purely on your description over the phone, are setting up the most common bait-and-switch in the industry: a low number to get you to book, followed by a much higher number once the truck is loaded and you have no real leverage.

Get quotes from at least three companies before you commit to one. Three numbers tell you where the real market rate sits for a move like yours; one number in isolation tells you nothing, and an unusually low quote won't look unusual until you have something to compare it against. If you want a sense of what pricing typically looks like for the Boston market specifically, our [full cost guide](/blog/boston-moving-cost-guide) breaks down what drives price up or down.

Video walkthroughs became normal after 2020 and most legitimate movers still offer them, which removes the old excuse of "we're too far away to send someone." A ten-minute video call where you walk through each room is enough for an experienced estimator to give you a number that actually holds up on move day, accounting for stairs, elevator access, and anything oversized that a text description alone would miss.

## The Deposit Test

A small deposit, typically capped around 20% of the estimated total, is standard practice. A mover demanding the full balance before your things are loaded, or one that only accepts cash with no receipt, is telling you exactly how much recourse you'll have if something goes wrong. You'll have none.

This connects directly to a bigger, uglier problem federal regulators have been actively cracking down on: hostage loads, where a crew loads your belongings, then refuses to unload them at the destination unless you pay a large, undocumented cash surcharge on the spot. It's illegal, and [FMCSA has pursued civil penalties of $10,000 or more per violation](https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/newsroom/fmcsa-continues-nationwide-crackdown-fraudulent-household-goods-movers-and-brokers) against companies caught doing it. It almost never happens with a company you vetted properly upfront. It happens to people who booked the cheapest number they found without asking a single follow-up question.

## Company or Broker? Ask Directly.

Not every business that quotes your move is the one that shows up to do it. A broker sells the job, then hires it out to whichever third-party carrier is available and cheapest, often an unfamiliar subcontractor with no track record you can check. Ask point blank: "Will your own crew and truck be doing this move, or are you brokering it to someone else?" A company with nothing to hide answers immediately.

| What to Ask | Good Answer | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Registration number | Provided immediately | Hesitation or refusal |
| How is the estimate done | In-home or video walkthrough | Phone quote only, no inspection |
| Deposit required | 20% or less | Full payment or large cash sum upfront |
| Who's doing the move | Their own crew and truck | Vague answer or "we'll assign someone" |
| Contract | Detailed, provided before move day | Blank, vague, or "we'll sort it out later" |

## How to Actually Verify a Company Before You Book

Anyone can put "licensed and insured" on a website. Verifying it takes about five minutes and it's worth doing before you hand over a deposit. For interstate movers, [FMCSA's SAFER database](https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/protect-your-move/select-mover) lets you look up a company's US DOT number and see its actual registration status, not just take their word for it. For local Massachusetts movers, the DPU maintains its own registry of movers it regulates.

Beyond the registration check, look up the company's actual business name (not just a brand name on a truck wrap) with the Secretary of the Commonwealth's corporate database to confirm it's a real, active entity and not a shell that changes names every season to dodge bad reviews. This sounds excessive for hiring a mover, but it takes less time than reading through a full page of reviews, and it catches a specific type of scam that reviews alone won't.

## Read the Contract Like You Mean It

A vague, incomplete, or missing written contract is one of the clearest signals a company is planning to add charges later that weren't part of the original conversation. The estimate, the valuation coverage you're getting (not just "insured," but which [specific tier of coverage](/blog/moving-insurance-boston) applies to your move), the pickup and delivery windows, and any stated fees should all be in writing before move day, not verbally promised and then absent from the paperwork.

If a company pressures you to sign immediately, or suddenly drops the price if you commit today instead of tomorrow, that pressure is doing a job: it's designed to stop you from reading the fine print or comparing it against another quote. A legitimate company doesn't need that tactic to win your business.

## What Actually Good Reviews Look Like

Reviews matter, but not all reviews are equally useful. A handful of generic five-star reviews with no specifics tells you less than a mix of detailed reviews that mention real crew names, specific neighborhoods, or how a company handled a problem when one came up. If you can't find any reviews at all for a company that claims to have been operating for years, that absence is itself the red flag.

For Boston specifically, check whether reviews mention experience with triple-deckers, walk-ups, and narrow streets, since [some neighborhoods are genuinely harder to move in and out of](/blog/hardest-boston-neighborhoods-to-move) than others, and a crew with real local experience handles those jobs differently than one seeing a Beacon Hill stairwell for the first time.
