Short answer: "fully insured" means a mover carries general liability, cargo, and workers' compensation coverage together, not just one policy covering your boxes. Here's what each piece actually protects, and why the distinction matters more than the phrase suggests.

## Insured Is Not the Same Thing as Licensed

These two words get used together so often that people assume they're one concept. They're not. Licensed means a mover is legally registered to operate, either through a US DOT number for interstate moves or DPU registration for moves within Massachusetts. Insured means they carry actual coverage for what can go wrong.

A mover can be licensed without carrying meaningful insurance, and in theory the reverse is possible too, though far less common in practice. [When you're vetting a company](/blog/how-to-choose-a-moving-company-boston), ask about both separately. "Are you licensed and insured" as a single question invites a single vague "yes" that doesn't actually tell you anything.

## The Three Policies Behind "Fully Insured"

"Fully insured" isn't one policy. It's shorthand for a package, and each piece covers a different risk:

- **General liability** covers damage a mover causes to property that isn't yours, a scraped hallway wall, a dented elevator door, a cracked stair tread, and covers third-party injury claims broadly.
- **Cargo insurance** (what the industry also calls valuation coverage) covers damage to your actual belongings while they're in the mover's possession.
- **Workers' compensation** covers the crew itself if someone gets injured on the job.

[Insurance providers who write these policies for the moving industry](https://www.insureon.com/trucking-business-insurance/moving-companies/cost) treat all three as standard components of a legitimate operation, not optional add-ons. A company skipping any one of them is cutting a real corner, even if their marketing still says "fully insured."

## Why General Liability Protects You, Not Just the Mover

This is the piece people underestimate. General liability isn't really about your belongings, it's about everything around your belongings: the building, the hallway, the elevator, the neighbor's parked car the truck backs into. If an uninsured crew damages your building's common area during a move, the building's own policy or your landlord's may end up covering it, with you potentially on the hook depending on your lease terms.

This is exactly why [Boston buildings request a certificate of insurance](/blog/moving-insurance-boston) before granting elevator or loading dock access. It's not paperwork for its own sake. It's the building confirming that if something gets damaged, the mover's policy pays for it instead of the building's.

## Workers' Compensation Is the Coverage Nobody Asks About

Almost nobody asks a mover about workers' comp, and almost everyone should. Moving is physical work with real injury risk: stairs, heavy furniture, awkward angles, hot summer days. If a crew member gets hurt on your property and the company doesn't carry workers' comp, the liability doesn't just disappear. Depending on the circumstances, it can potentially extend to the property owner.

A [company carrying proper workers' comp](https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/consumer-protection/protect-your-move/are-you-moving/liability-protection) keeps that risk exactly where it belongs, with the employer. It's one more reason a cheap, uninsured operator isn't actually the cheap option once you account for what you're personally exposed to if something goes wrong.

## Cargo Insurance: The One Piece Most People Do Ask About

Cargo insurance, or valuation coverage, is the piece that covers your actual belongings, and it's the one most people think of first when they hear "insured." But even a fully insured company's baseline cargo coverage is limited. The default tier, released value protection, reimburses at a flat $0.60 per pound per item, regardless of what the item actually cost.

That's a real gap for anyone moving electronics, real furniture, or anything valuable relative to its weight. We've [written a full breakdown of released value versus full value protection](/blog/moving-insurance-boston) if you want the exact numbers and how to decide whether the upgrade is worth it for your move.

## How to Actually Verify Coverage Instead of Trusting a Website Claim

Anyone can write "fully insured" on a homepage. Verifying it takes one direct question: ask for a certificate of insurance (COI). A real COI lists the specific insurance carrier, the policy numbers, and the coverage limits for each type of policy. It's a standard document any legitimately insured company can produce within a day or two, and most keep a template ready to send.

If you want more certainty, the COI includes the carrier's name and contact information, and you can call to confirm the policy is active. Most people never take this extra step, but it's available if the size or stakes of your move justify the extra five minutes.

| Coverage Type | What It Protects | Who It Protects |
|---|---|---|
| General Liability | Property damage beyond your items (walls, elevators, building) | You, the building, third parties |
| Cargo / Valuation | Your actual belongings | You |
| Workers' Compensation | Injured crew members | The crew, indirectly you |

## A Realistic Scenario, Not a Hypothetical One

Picture a routine job: a crew is carrying a dresser down a narrow triple-decker stairwell in Somerville, and it clips the wall on a tight turn, taking a chunk of plaster with it. With a properly insured company, this is a minor, well-handled inconvenience: the mover's general liability policy covers the wall repair, you note it on the paperwork, and everyone moves on with the job.

Without that coverage, the same scraped wall becomes a dispute. The mover may deny responsibility, offer a token amount out of pocket, or simply disappear after the job is done and stop answering calls. You're left negotiating repair costs with your landlord or building management on your own, with no policy backing you up and no real leverage to make it right. The item itself was never the risk in that scenario. The building was, and that's exactly the layer "fully insured" is supposed to cover.

## What This Doesn't Guarantee

Being fully insured doesn't mean a move goes perfectly, and it doesn't mean every possible loss is automatically covered at full value. It means that if something does go wrong, there's a real financial backstop instead of an empty promise. That's the actual value of the phrase, once you understand what's behind it: not a guarantee of a flawless move, but a guarantee that someone besides you is on the hook if it isn't.
