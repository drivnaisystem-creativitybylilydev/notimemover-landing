Short answer: the scams that hit Massachusetts movers follow a small number of predictable patterns, lowball quotes, hostage loads, and shell companies that keep changing names. Here's how each one actually works and how to spot it before you're the one dealing with it.

## The Lowball Quote, Then the Add-On

This is the most common pattern by far. A quote comes in noticeably lower than every other estimate you got, you book based on that number, and once your things are loaded, new charges appear that weren't part of the original conversation, stairs, "extra weight," a longer drive than estimated, items that suddenly need "special handling." Individually each charge sounds almost plausible. Together, they add up to a bill nowhere near what you were quoted.

The defense here is the one covered in [how to choose a moving company](/blog/how-to-choose-a-moving-company-boston): get multiple quotes, insist on an in-home or video estimate instead of a phone-only number, and get everything in writing before move day. A number that seems too good to be true against two or three other quotes almost always is.

## Hostage Loads: The Scam That Escalates Past Money

A hostage load is the most serious pattern, and it's exactly what it sounds like. A crew loads your belongings, drives to the destination, and then refuses to unload unless you pay a large, undocumented cash sum on the spot, effectively holding your possessions until you comply. It's not a gray area or a pricing dispute. It's illegal under federal law, and [FMCSA has pursued civil penalties of $10,000 or more per violation](https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/newsroom/fmcsa-continues-nationwide-crackdown-fraudulent-household-goods-movers-and-brokers) against companies caught running this exact scheme.

This scenario is why the earlier vetting steps matter so much. It practically never happens with a company you verified against FMCSA's SAFER database or the DPU's registry ahead of time. It happens almost exclusively with unverified, cash-only operators booked off a lowball quote with no paper trail.

## Shell Companies: Same Operator, New Name

Some operators run a company, accumulate a trail of complaints or bad reviews, shut it down, and reopen under a nearly identical name with the same trucks, the same crew, and the same underlying business, betting that a fresh name means a clean slate online. [Massachusetts residents report this pattern](https://www.moverstotrust.com/scams/ma) to federal authorities with some regularity, and it's one of the harder scams to catch just by reading reviews, since the bad history is attached to a name that technically no longer exists.

The fix is checking actual business registration, not just a company's current marketing. The Secretary of the Commonwealth's corporate database and FMCSA's SAFER lookup both show real registration history, which a rebrand alone can't hide.

## Brokers Posing as Carriers

Not every scam is outright fraud. Some are closer to a bait-and-switch: a broker takes your booking and payment, then hires out the actual move to whatever third-party subcontractor is cheapest and available that day, often with no crew vetting and no guarantee of quality. You booked based on one company's reputation and got a completely different, unknown operator showing up at your door.

Ask directly whether the company quoting you will be the one physically doing the move, or whether they're brokering it out. A legitimate broker will tell you plainly. One trying to obscure it usually won't.

## Cash-Only Payment Demands

A cash-only requirement, especially combined with any other red flag on this list, removes your paper trail entirely. No receipt, no card statement, no documented proof of what you paid or agreed to if a dispute comes up later. It's not automatically proof of a scam on its own, plenty of small operators genuinely prefer cash for mundane reasons, but paired with pressure to pay the full amount upfront, it's a combination worth walking away from.

## Where to Report a Scam

If you've been targeted, file a complaint with FMCSA at nccdb.fmcsa.dot.gov or call 1-888-DOT-SAFT for interstate moves. For a purely local Massachusetts move, the state DPU handles complaints against the movers it regulates directly. Reporting doesn't just help your own situation, it builds the record that federal and state enforcement actions against [rogue movers and brokers](https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/newsroom/fmcsa-launches-operation-protect-your-move-nationwide-crackdown-moving-scams) actually rely on.

## Why Boston's Rental Market Makes This Worse

A city with as much annual turnover as Boston, driven heavily by students and the [September 1 lease cycle](/blog/moving-september-1-boston), is a predictable target for seasonal scam operators. They know a huge number of people are booking a mover for the first time, on a tight deadline, often comparing prices under real time pressure. That combination, inexperience plus urgency, is exactly what these scams are built to exploit.

If this is your first time hiring a mover in the city, budget extra time for the vetting steps above rather than treating them as optional. The pressure to book quickly during peak season is real, but it's also precisely the pressure a lowball scam depends on to work.

## What a Legitimate Company Looks Like By Comparison

None of this should make hiring a mover feel like a minefield. The overwhelming majority of moving companies, including in a market as saturated as Boston, are legitimate, insured, and straightforward to verify. A real company answers questions about registration and insurance directly, provides a written contract without resistance, and doesn't pressure you to sign or pay before you're ready. If a company clears all of that without hesitation, the odds of running into any of the patterns above drop close to zero.

## The Pattern Behind All of These

Every scam on this list depends on the same thing: skipping verification. A verified registration number, an in-home estimate, a written contract, and a small, reasonable deposit close off nearly every scam pattern here at once. It's a five-minute check against a mistake that can cost thousands and take weeks to untangle.
