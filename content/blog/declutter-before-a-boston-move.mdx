Start 4-6 weeks out, and be ruthless with anything you haven't touched in a year. Decluttering before a move is the single easiest way to shrink both your bill and your moving day.

## Why Decluttering Before a Move Beats Doing It After

Almost everyone plans to sort things out once they're unpacked in the new place. It rarely happens that way. The box of stuff you meant to deal with "eventually" gets carried up three flights, shoved in a closet, and dealt with a year later during the next move instead.

That's paid labor spent moving something you were always going to get rid of. On a local Boston move, [pricing tracks the inventory and hours a crew needs](/blog/hidden-moving-fees-boston), not just distance. A smaller, tighter load often means a smaller crew or less time on site, both of which show up directly in what you pay.

We build every NoTimeMover quote around the inventory a customer gives us upfront, and that number gets more accurate, and often lower, the more someone has already cleared out before we show up. It also means less physical work for the crew hauling a dresser nobody wants down a narrow triple-decker stairwell, which matters more than people expect once you've watched it happen a few times.

## When to Start: The 4-6 Week Window

[MoveBuddha's decluttering guide](https://www.movebuddha.com/blog/decluttering-for-a-move/) recommends starting the process 4-6 weeks before move day, working through storage areas first and saving the rooms you use daily for last. That order matters. Basements, attics, and closets hold the stuff you've genuinely forgotten about, so they're the easiest place to build momentum before you hit anything sentimental.

Here's a simple breakdown to work from:

| Timeframe | What to Tackle |
|---|---|
| 6 weeks out | Basement, attic, garage, storage closets |
| 4 weeks out | Bedrooms, living room, home office |
| 2 weeks out | Kitchen, bathroom, everyday-use items |
| Final week | Anything left in the "maybe" pile, plus final donation drop-offs |

Waiting until the final week to start means every decision gets made under time pressure, and time pressure is exactly when people end up paying to move things they didn't actually want.

## The Four-Bin Method (Keep, Sell, Donate, Trash)

Label four bins, boxes, or corners of a room: Keep, Sell, Donate, Trash. Go through one space at a time and force every item into one of the four, no "I'll decide later" pile allowed.

The rule that actually works: if you haven't used it in the past year and it's not seasonal or sentimental, it goes. Kitchen gadgets, cables for devices you no longer own, and clothes that don't fit are usually the fastest wins, since there's rarely much internal debate once you're honest about it.

For the Sell bin, Facebook Marketplace moves faster in Boston than a garage sale for most furniture and electronics, especially with move-in season traffic around September. For anything genuinely valuable, price it to move quickly rather than holding out for top dollar. A piece of furniture still in your apartment on move day is a piece of furniture you're paying to transport.

## Where Does Boston Actually Let You Get Rid of Things?

Regular trash and recycling handle small stuff on your normal collection day. Furniture, mattresses, and large appliances are a different process entirely.

Boston requires an appointment for special collection items, scheduled through [BOS:311](https://www.boston.gov/departments/public-works/special-collection-items). You're allowed up to 10 special-collection items per calendar year, with a maximum of 5 per appointment, and each item has to stay under 250 pounds and 7 feet long. Mattresses and appliances with refrigerants or CRT screens specifically require a confirmed appointment before you set them out. Skip the appointment and the item simply won't get collected.

Around September 1, you'll see the informal version of this all over Allston, Brighton, and Somerville: usable furniture left at the curb with a handwritten "free" sign, picked up by whoever walks by first. It's a real Boston tradition, but it's not an official disposal method, and it's not guaranteed. Furniture that sits too long unclaimed can turn into a fine, so don't treat the curb as a backup plan for anything you actually need gone by a certain date.

## Who Picks Up Donations in Boston?

[Goodwill's Massachusetts locations](https://www.goodwillmass.org/donation-center-hours-locations/) offer furniture pickup for donors across eastern and central Massachusetts, in addition to drop-off at their donation centers, which typically run 10am-7pm on weekdays and Saturday, with shorter Sunday hours. Donations are tax-deductible, and most locations will give you a receipt on request.

Salvation Army and several smaller local charities also run pickup programs, though availability and lead time vary more than Goodwill's. Book a pickup a week or two out if you're aiming for a specific move date, since donation trucks fill up fast during the late-August and early-September rush when half the city is clearing out apartments at once.

If you're dealing with a full basement or attic clear-out that's more trash than usable furniture, a junk removal service is usually faster than stacking up multiple 311 appointments, even though it isn't free.

## What Shouldn't You Declutter Too Early?

Hold onto anything you'll need in the first 48 hours at the new place before you get to donating or tossing it: medications, chargers, a few days of clothes, basic toiletries, and any documents you'd panic without, like a lease, passport, or medical records.

Also hold off on selling furniture you're unsure will actually fit. Measure doorways, hallways, and stairwells at the new address before you decide a couch won't work, especially in Boston's older buildings where a tight stairwell or a walk-up with no elevator can rule out replacements that would've fit fine somewhere else. Replacing something you sold too fast, on top of moving costs you're already paying, defeats the purpose of decluttering in the first place. If you're not sure yet, [temporary storage](/blog/boston-storage-options-during-a-move) is cheaper than re-buying.

## Does Decluttering Actually Lower Your Moving Quote?

It depends on how the move is priced, but the answer is yes either way.

| Move Type | How It's Priced | Effect of Decluttering |
|---|---|---|
| Local Boston move | Crew size, hours, truck size | Lighter load can mean fewer crew hours or a smaller truck |
| Interstate / long-distance move | Shipment weight | Every pound you don't ship is a direct reduction off the bill |

For a [local move](/local-moving), the crew and truck get sized around what you report, so a smaller inventory tends to translate into a tighter number, not just a faster day. For anyone [moving out of state from Boston](/blog/moving-out-of-state-from-boston), the math is even more direct: carriers weigh the truck before and after loading, and price is built almost entirely around that number. Nothing you leave behind gets billed.

There's a smaller, easy-to-miss lever too. A lighter load sometimes means a smaller truck, and a smaller truck needs less curb space. That matters on narrow Boston streets, where crews already have to file for a [no-parking permit](/blog/boston-moving-truck-parking-permit) ahead of time. A 20-foot truck reserving a short stretch of curb is a smaller ask than one that needs the whole block, and it's one less thing that can go wrong on move morning.
