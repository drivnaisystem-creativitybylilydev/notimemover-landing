Short answer: update the microchip, gather vet records, keep your pet in one quiet room away from the moving path, and maintain their normal routine as much as the day allows. Here's the full picture for a Boston move specifically.

## Confirm Pet Policies Before You Sign, Not After

Boston's rental market varies widely on pets, breed and weight restrictions, one-time pet deposits, ongoing monthly pet rent, and building-specific rules that aren't always listed clearly online. Confirm the actual policy directly with the landlord or property manager before you sign anything, and get any verbal pet approval written into the lease itself. A landlord's spoken "yes, pets are fine" that never makes it into the paperwork isn't something you want to be relying on if a dispute comes up later.

## Update the Microchip. Today, Not Move Week.

This is the single most skipped step, and it's the one that matters most exactly when things go wrong. [Almost nobody remembers to update a pet's microchip registry](https://petharmonytraining.com/7-tips-for-moving-with-pets/) before a move, and moving day itself, with doors propped open and unfamiliar people moving in and out constantly, is one of the highest-risk moments for a pet to slip out unnoticed. Log into your chip's registry now and update the address and contact info, so if the worst happens, whoever finds your pet can actually reach you at your current information.

## Get Vet Records Before You Leave Your Current Vet

Request copies of your pet's full medical and vaccination records while you're still an active client at your current clinic. [Tracking records down weeks or months after you've moved](https://www.fbfs.com/learning-center/6-essential-tips-for-moving-with-pets-with-checklist) is a genuinely annoying process involving faxes, callbacks, and delays, especially if you're establishing care with a new vet in your new neighborhood and they need proof of prior vaccinations. Get everything in hand before the move, digital or paper, so it's ready the moment you need it.

## Build the Carrier Association Early

If your pet isn't already comfortable with a carrier, start building a positive association well before move day. [Leave the carrier out with the door open, feed meals inside it, and toss in treats](https://www.prettyfluffy.com/beyond/moving-with-pets-guide) so it becomes a familiar, non-threatening object instead of something that only appears right before something stressful happens. For cats especially, short practice car rides in the weeks leading up to the move help reduce anxiety about vehicle travel when moving day actually arrives.

## Moving Day: One Room, One Sign, One Plan

On the day itself, pick one quiet room away from the main path movers are using, set up your pet's bed, food, water, and a familiar toy, and put a clear "Pet Inside, Please Keep Door Closed" sign on it. This matters even more in a Boston walk-up or triple-decker, where a narrow stairwell and a front door propped open for hours of carrying boxes is a real bolting risk that a suburban home with a fenced yard doesn't have in the same way.

Walk your dog right before the crew arrives, so they start the noisiest, busiest part of the day already a little tired instead of full of pent-up energy. For cats, closing them into the designated room before movers arrive, rather than trying to corral them once the door's already open and people are moving in and out, saves a lot of stress for everyone involved.

## Should You Board Them Instead?

For a pet with real anxiety around noise, strangers, or disruption, a day at a sitter's or a boarding facility away from the move entirely can be the better call, not a failure to "handle it yourself." [If anxiety is severe](https://www.trupanion.com/pet-blog/article/moving-with-pets), talk to your vet ahead of time, since situational anxiety medication is a real option for a single stressful day like this, prescribed specifically for events like a move rather than ongoing daily use.

## Pet Fees Are Part of Your Real Moving Budget

Pet deposits and monthly pet rent are easy to forget when you're budgeting a move, since they're a housing cost rather than a moving cost, but they land at the same time as everything else. Add them into your overall moving budget the same way you'd account for [other easy-to-miss costs](/blog/hidden-moving-fees-boston), so the total picture is accurate before you commit to a new place.

## Stairs, Triple-Deckers, and Pet Safety

Some Boston buildings genuinely make pet logistics harder than others. A third-floor walk-up with a narrow, angled staircase means more trips up and down for you, more time the front door sits open, and more opportunities for a nervous pet to slip past someone carrying a box. If you already know [your building or neighborhood tends toward tighter, harder-to-navigate stairwells](/blog/hardest-boston-neighborhoods-to-move), plan the closed-room strategy even more carefully, and consider having a second person dedicated just to keeping an eye on your pet during the busiest loading window.

## Maintain Routine Everywhere Else

Amid all the disruption, keep whatever routine you can control exactly the same: same feeding times, same walk schedule, same bedtime. [Predictability is what actually reduces stress](https://vetster.com/en/wellness/moving-guide-for-pet-owners-how-to-move-with-pets) for most pets during a period where nearly everything else in their environment is changing at once. It's a small thing that costs nothing and genuinely helps.
