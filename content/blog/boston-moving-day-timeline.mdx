Short answer: 6 to 10 hours, start to finish, for a typical local move. Here's what happens during each one, and where a Boston move tends to lose time that a move in a suburban driveway-and-garage neighborhood usually doesn't.

## The Boston Moving Day Timeline, Hour by Hour

Every move is different, but a standard local move with a morning start follows a predictable shape. Here's what a 2-bedroom apartment move typically looks like from a Boston street or triple-decker.

| Time | What's Happening |
|---|---|
| 7:00 – 8:00 AM | Final prep: essentials box packed, beds stripped, pathways cleared, permit signs already posted |
| 8:00 – 8:30 AM | Crew arrives, does a walkthrough, checks in with the building or handles COI/elevator logistics |
| 8:30 – 11:30 AM | Loading the truck (roughly 2-3 hours for a 2-bedroom with a 2-3 person crew) |
| 11:30 AM – 12:30 PM | Drive time and parking at the new address |
| 12:30 – 3:30 PM | Unloading and furniture placement |
| 3:30 – 4:00 PM | Final walkthrough, payment, and tip |

Studios and 1-bedrooms compress this. Larger homes and long-distance moves stretch it. Use it as the shape of the day, then adjust the middle blocks for your own square footage.

## What Should You Do in the Two Hours Before Your Crew Arrives?

Everything should already be packed. Movers loading boxes is efficient; movers standing around while you finish taping a box is not, and if you're paying by the hour, that time comes straight out of your budget.

Strip the beds, clear a straight path from each room to the door, and set your essentials box aside where it won't get loaded by mistake. That box should hold toiletries, chargers, medications, important documents, and a change of clothes, so your first night doesn't depend on finding one specific box in a stack of forty. [Move.org's mover-prep guide](https://www.move.org/prepare-for-movers/) covers this same ground in more detail if you want a full pre-move checklist.

If you filed a street parking permit, confirm the signs are actually up. They typically go up 48 hours ahead, and if they're not visible the morning of, that's worth a phone call before the truck shows up, not after. We've written a full breakdown of [how the Boston moving permit process works](/blog/boston-moving-truck-parking-permit) if you haven't filed yet.

## Crew Arrival and the Walkthrough

The crew lead walks the space with you, confirms what's going and what's staying, and flags anything that needs special handling, like a piano, a mirror, or an oversized sectional that won't clear a stairwell turn. This is also when you point out the "do not load" pile, so nothing you meant to keep ends up on the truck.

For building moves, this is when the crew checks in with the front desk or super, confirms the freight elevator reservation, and hands over the certificate of insurance if the building requires one. Skipping this step is the single most common reason a move stalls before it even starts.

Triple-deckers and walk-ups add their own version of this same delay: narrow stairwells, tight turns on the landing, and doorframes that weren't built with a modern sofa in mind. We've covered which [Boston neighborhoods tend to be hardest to move in and out of](/blog/hardest-boston-neighborhoods-to-move) if you want to know what to expect before crew even arrives.

## Loading: Where Most of the Day Actually Goes

Loading is the biggest single block of the day, and it scales directly with home size. Big furniture and appliances go first for weight distribution, then boxes, then anything fragile that needs to ride on top or get wedged in last.

| Home Size | Typical Loading Time | Typical Total Move Time |
|---|---|---|
| Studio / 1BR | 1-2 hours | 2-4 hours |
| 2BR | 2-3 hours | 3-6 hours |
| 3BR | 3-4 hours | 5-8 hours |
| 4BR+ | 4-5+ hours | 8-10+ hours |

Figures are for a local move with ground-floor or working elevator access, per [moveBuddha's move-time data](https://www.movebuddha.com/blog/how-long-does-it-take-to-move/). Add time for stairs, long carries from a double-parked truck, or a building that only allows freight elevator access during a narrow window.

## Do You Need an Elevator Reservation or COI on Moving Day?

If you're in a high-rise or a mid-size building with six or more units, probably. Property managers use the certificate of insurance to protect common areas and hallways from damage, and the freight elevator reservation keeps your move from colliding with someone else's on the same day.

Get both sorted two to three days out, not the morning of. Buildings that require a COI generally won't grant elevator or loading dock access without one already on file, and a reservation slot that's already booked by another tenant isn't something you can argue your way into on move day. If your building doesn't have a freight elevator at all, factor that into how long the carry will take, since every trip is now a full flight of stairs instead of a thirty-second elevator ride.

We build buffer into every Boston quote for buildings that need a reservation or a COI, because losing forty minutes waiting on a super to unlock the freight elevator is common enough on these jobs that treating it as a rare exception would be unrealistic. Locking in your budget before move day means a slow elevator or a building with extra paperwork doesn't turn into a surprise hourly overage on your final invoice.

## Transit and Arrival at the New Place

Drive time depends entirely on distance and traffic, but budget more than a maps app tells you. A moving truck is slower through the city, harder to park, and more likely to need a second pass around the block if the curb is occupied when you arrive. Midday traffic through the Seaport, Back Bay, or anywhere near the Pike can eat twenty minutes that a straight-line distance estimate never accounted for.

If you filed a permit at the new address too, confirm the window lines up with your realistic arrival time, not your optimistic one. A parking permit that opens at 8 AM does you no good if loading ran long and the truck doesn't roll up until noon. If the reserved spot is taken or the signs never went up, the crew will need to find legal parking nearby and carry everything farther, which adds real time to a leg of the day most people don't plan for at all.

Two-address moves, where both the old and new place are in Boston, compound this. Each address needs its own permit and its own realistic time window, and the gap between "loading finished" and "truck parked at the new curb" is where a tight schedule usually slips.

## What Happens During the Final Walkthrough?

Two walkthroughs matter, one at each end. At the old place, open every closet, cabinet, and drawer, and check under sinks, behind doors, and in the basement or any storage area, since these are the spots people forget most often. Confirm with the crew lead that the truck is empty of anything that shouldn't be there.

At the new place, do a count against your inventory before the truck leaves. This is the moment to flag anything missing or damaged, while the crew is still on-site and able to address it, rather than discovering it after they've driven off. Photograph anything that looks damaged before it gets moved into place against a wall or under other boxes, since that photo is what backs up a claim later if you need one.

Sign whatever paperwork the crew hands you only after that count is done, not before. Once you're satisfied, that's the point to [tip the crew](/blog/how-much-to-tip-movers-boston) if the job went well.

## How Do You Keep Your Timeline From Running Long?

Most delays trace back to the same handful of causes: packing that wasn't finished, boxes that weren't labeled, a permit or elevator reservation that wasn't confirmed ahead of time, or a truck that can't get close enough to the door. None of these are about the crew's speed. They're about prep.

Finish packing the night before, not the morning of. Label boxes by room so placement at the new address doesn't require guessing. Confirm your parking permit and any building requirements two to three days out. And if you're moving on or near [September 1](/blog/moving-september-1-boston), build in extra time everywhere, since crews are running back-to-back jobs and buildings are handling more move traffic than any other day of the year.
